Week 3

Ways of seeing

Written Reading Response

Images have an encoded meaning ready to be decoded. Whether or not the author intended any meaning to arise from a creation, humans love to decode. Everything exists within a contextual setting. Nothing exists within a vacuum. The image is no exception, especially since we string together our sensory input into one collective experience to comprehend.

Similarly, art is set free and goes whatever the wind carries it. By wind, I mean the audience or user, and by it, I mean whatever is created and intended for consumption. Unintended consequences are the beauty of art. The meaning lies within the eyes of the beholder if you will. The author discusses the way in which we gather the fragments of the world's information. About the work, we put on ourselves to make sense of the world. We string together bits and pieces of images, text, sounds, and other types of sensory input. Then, magically, we experience a second as a collection of things all happening in harmony. Unfortunately, that objective viewpoint we think we possess is far from objective. We, with all our existing biases and preconceived notions, arrive at the data set presented before us as anything but a blank slate. This hardwiring is like the code that is running through the variables of life. I got pretty philosophical, but the magic of our senses never ceases to amaze me.




Week 2

As we may think
Long live the web

Written Reading Response

The web is a public space. It is a digital world. This mini-world that fits in the back of your pocket feels so personal at times because of how we can access it. Our every step is captured on the web. Every breath we take, every search we click, every move we make is seen even if we are not aware of it. Tim Burners Lee discusses how critical it is to maintain free speech in this world. Democratizing the web and keeping this world free from censorship feels like a Utopia, but at the very least, we must keep this world free from the Dystopia that is just as near.

Bush speaks about the technological gadgets we use today in this utopian way. Strange to step back to 1945 and hear how it felt to experience the radical shift. The exponential curve of innovation pushed scientists to reimagine how they define themselves and how they define the world around us. This Utopia is here; it is UPON US. Even though it technically runs on a series of Laws, known as the Internet Protocols, Lee speaks about the importance of including human rights into the laws of the web. Something so obvious today that was not a huge topic of conversation at the dawn of the web led to the modern-day world we live in. Imagine how different the web could have been if only more conversations were had about human-centric design. As we put ourselves in time, we are right at the turning point of technology. Here in 2021, we can have this self-awareness within the looking glass. We can perceive the future consequences of our actions. We can take control of our futures (for the most part). With this newfound power and self-awareness, we continue to develop faster than ever. Now the focus on creating technology to protect people and maintain human rights is very real. The human experience is fragile and ever-changing. Designing in a human-centric vision leads to a better world not linearly but exponentially.
